02 / 08
What gets lost

You live with your body 24/7. Your doctor sees it for fifteen minutes.

Between visits, the story of how you actually felt — the rough nights, the stressful weeks, the quietly good days — stays with you. It rarely reaches the appointment in a form either of you can use.

The appointment

A few minutes

Of memory-driven symptoms, shared under pressure, in a room you barely know.

The in-between

Weeks of small signals

Voice notes, sleep, mood, stress — lived through, but never quite written down.

The gap

Well-meaning, disconnected

No one is at fault. The loop between living and reporting is just broken.

03 / 08
The idea

A companion invited for a purpose, then let go.

Not another app. A temporary sidecar, offered through a small protocol, that lives inside your personal agent for as long as it’s useful — and leaves when you’re done.

1

Invite

A doctor or template offers a sidecar tuned to a question.

2

Consent

You grant read-only access to specific data. Nothing more.

3

Observe

It notices patterns — each one traceable to the notes behind it.

4

Let go

A TTL, an audit log, and a revoke button. You stay in control.

05 / 08
What it does, today

Patterns you can trace back to a source.

A working prototype you can run locally. It finds patterns and cites the notes behind them. It can explore a what-if — what if sleep steadied for two weeks? — by looking at your own good and poor periods, not a population average.

Running locally vitaside · bash 
# invite a sidecar
$ ./issue-sidecar.sh sleep-stress-sidecar

# find patterns — each cited to a dated note
$ python3 run_demo_check.py analyze
→ patterns found · linked to source notes

# explore a what-if from your own history
$ python3 run_demo_check.py whatif
→ contrasts your good vs poor periods

# a report to bring to the visit
$ python3 run_demo_check.py html

Your data doesn’t travel to a server to be understood. The sidecar reads locally, writes an audit log of every file it touched, and stops when its time is up.
